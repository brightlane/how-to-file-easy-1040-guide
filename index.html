PART 1 — Your Personal Information

📋 Personal Information

Name & Address Your Full Name, Address & SSN 🏠

The very top of Form 1040 asks for your full legal name (first, middle initial, last), your current home address, city, state, and ZIP code. This is also where you enter your Social Security Number (SSN) — or Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN) if you don't have an SSN.

What You Need

  • Your Social Security card (to double-check your SSN)
  • Your current mailing address
  • Spouse's name and SSN if filing jointly
Professor Owl Says:
"Your SSN is like your tax ID fingerprint — one wrong digit and the IRS can't find you! Triple-check this number. It's the most common mistake I see beginners make."
If your name doesn't exactly match what's on file with the Social Security Administration (SSA), your return could be delayed!
Filing Status Check Your Filing Status — How Do You File? 👨‍👩‍👧

You must choose ONE filing status. This affects your tax rates and deductions — so pick carefully!

The 5 Filing Statuses

  • Single — Unmarried, or legally separated
  • Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) — Married and combining your incomes (usually best!)
  • Married Filing Separately (MFS) — Married but keeping taxes separate
  • Head of Household (HOH) — Unmarried and paying over half the cost of a home for a dependent
  • Qualifying Surviving Spouse — Widowed in past 2 years with a dependent child
Professor Owl Says:
"Most first-timers are Single — just check that box! If you're married, Married Filing Jointly almost always gives you a bigger refund. Head of Household is great for single parents — don't miss it if you qualify!"
Dependents Who Do You Support? List Your Dependents 👶

A dependent is someone who relies on you financially — usually a child under 19 (or under 24 if a full-time student), or a qualifying relative you support.

What You Need

  • Dependent's full name and Social Security Number
  • Their relationship to you (son, daughter, parent, etc.)
  • Check the box: "Child Tax Credit" or "Other Dependent"
Professor Owl Says:
"Each qualifying child can get you up to $2,000 in Child Tax Credit — that's real money back in your pocket! Never skip listing your kids."
Only one parent can claim a child per year. If you're divorced or separated, make sure you and your ex agree on who claims the kids!
