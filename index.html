📋 Part 1: Filing Status

The very first question on your tax form: "Are you single, married, or something else?" This one choice changes how much tax you pay! Let Professor Hootsworth explain each one.

🦉 Professor Hootsworth says:

▶ HOOT! Pick just ONE box! Your filing status is like your "tax category." It changes your standard deduction, your tax bracket — everything!

Most first-time filers? Check SINGLE. It's the most common for young folks! 🎯

☐ SINGLE — Most First-Time Filers!
You are NOT married as of December 31 of the tax year
Even if you have a boyfriend/girlfriend, lived with someone, or have a roommate — if you weren't legally married on December 31, you're SINGLE for taxes. Simple!
📌 You're 22, have a job, not married → Check "Single" ✓
☐ Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)
You AND your spouse file ONE return together
You were legally married by December 31. Filing together usually saves the most money — you get a bigger standard deduction and better tax rates!
📌 Got married in June → file jointly with your spouse for the whole year
☐ Married Filing Separately (MFS)
Married but filing your own return
Usually costs MORE in taxes. Only use this if there's a specific reason (like you can't agree on a joint return, or you need to protect yourself from your spouse's tax debt).
☐ Head of Household (HOH)
Single + you support a child in your home
You're NOT married AND you paid more than half the cost of a home where you AND a qualifying child lived for more than half the year. You get BETTER tax rates than regular Single!
📌 Single mom supporting her kids → Head of Household
☐ Qualifying Surviving Spouse
Your spouse passed away in the last 2 years
You have a dependent child and your spouse died in one of the past two tax years. You get the same low tax rates as Married Filing Jointly.
🤔 Not sure which one? Answer these: Are you married? → MFJ or MFS. Single with a kid you support? → Head of Household. None of the above? → Single!
  • You MUST pick one filing status — no skipping this!
  • Most first-timers: check Single
  • Single parent supporting kids? Check Head of Household — it's better!
  • Your status must match your situation on December 31 of the tax year

📋 Part 2: Your Personal Information

This part is basically filling out your name tag — but for the IRS. It sounds easy, and it IS... but one wrong digit in your Social Security Number causes huge delays. Triple-check!

Your Social Security Number (SSN) ← THE MOST IMPORTANT THING TO GET RIGHT!
9 digits, looks like: 123-45-6789. This is how the IRS knows it's YOU. One wrong digit = your return gets rejected or delayed by MONTHS!
Your Legal Name
Write your name EXACTLY as it appears on your Social Security card. No nicknames! If the IRS can't match your name to your SSN, your return bounces back.
Your Address
Use your current mailing address — even if you moved this year. This is where the IRS will mail you anything important.
Dependents
People you financially support (usually your children)
For each dependent, you need: their full name, their SSN, their relationship to you (daughter, son, etc.), and whether they qualify for the Child Tax Credit.
📌 To claim your child: they must live with you 6+ months and be under 19 (or under 24 if a full-time student)
⚠️ BIG MISTAKE ALERT! Never claim a child someone else already claimed! The IRS computers catch this instantly and both returns get flagged!
  • Triple-check your Social Security Number — wrong = delays!
  • Use your name exactly as on your Social Security card
  • Each dependent needs their own SSN — get it from their Social Security card

💰 Part 3: Your Income (Lines 1–8)

Now we add up ALL the money you made this year. From your job, from the bank, from selling stuff — EVERYTHING. The IRS wants the full picture!

  • W-2 wages from your job (Line 1a)
  • Interest from bank accounts (Line 2b)
  • Dividends from investments (Line 3b)
  • Money from gig work, side hustles (via Schedule 1)
  • The BIG TOTAL: your gross income (Line 9)
🦉 Professor Hootsworth says:

▶ Count up ALL your money! Think of it like emptying your piggy bank and counting every coin. From your job, from the bank — everything goes here!

🎯 Start with Line 1a — that's your W-2 Box 1. For most people, that's the BIG one!

Line 1a — W-2 Box 1 Wages START HERE!
Your main job pay (before taxes were taken out)
Look at your W-2 form from your employer. Find the box labeled "Box 1 — Wages, tips, other compensation." Copy that number here. Had TWO jobs? Add both W-2 Box 1 amounts together!
📌 Your W-2 Box 1 says $38,000 → Write $38,000 on Line 1a
Line 1c — Tips Not on Your W-2
Cash tips you received as a server, bartender, or delivery driver that weren't included in your W-2 Box 1. ALL tips are taxable — yes, even cash!
Line 1z — Total Wages
Add up all the Line 1 parts (1a + 1b + 1c + ...)
📌 Line 1a = $38,000 + cash tips $500 = Line 1z = $38,500

Other Income Types

Line 2b — Taxable Interest (from your bank)
Interest your savings account or CDs paid you. Find it on Form 1099-INT Box 1 from your bank. Even $50 from a savings account counts! If total interest is over $1,500, also fill out Schedule B.
📌 Your bank paid you $85 in interest → Line 2b = $85
Line 3b — Ordinary Dividends (from stocks)
Dividends your stocks or investment funds paid you. From Form 1099-DIV Box 1a. Over $1,500? Also fill Schedule B.
Line 4b — Taxable IRA / Pension Distributions
Money you took OUT of a retirement account (IRA, 401k). The taxable amount is from Form 1099-R Box 2a.
⚠️ Under 59½ and took money from your IRA? You'll usually pay a 10% early withdrawal PENALTY too! Professor Hootsworth says: leave that retirement money alone! 🦉
Line 7 — Capital Gains or Losses (from Schedule D)
Sold stocks, cryptocurrency, or real estate? Your profit or loss from Schedule D goes here.
📌 Bought stock for $2,000, sold for $3,000 → $1,000 gain
Line 9 — TOTAL INCOME BIG TOTAL!
Everything added up
Add Lines 1z + 2b + 3b + 4b + 5b + 6b + 7 + 8. This is your grand total income for the year. But don't panic — we're about to subtract a LOT from it!
📌 $38,500 wages + $85 interest = $38,585 total income
  • Line 1a = your W-2 Box 1 (your job wages)
  • Had two jobs? Add BOTH W-2 Box 1 amounts together
  • ALL income counts — bank interest, dividends, tips, side gigs, everything
  • Line 9 = your big total. Don't worry, we reduce it next!

📉 Part 4: Adjustments to Income (Line 10)

Good news! Before we calculate your tax, we get to SUBTRACT some things from your income. These "adjustments" are like coupons — they reduce how much of your income gets taxed!

  • Student loan interest (up to $2,500 off!)
  • IRA contributions
  • These come from Schedule 1, Part II
Line 10 — Adjustments to Income (Schedule 1, Part II)
These "above-the-line" deductions reduce your income BEFORE you even get to the standard deduction. The best part? You can take them AND the standard deduction! Common ones: student loan interest, IRA deduction, self-employed health insurance.
🦉 Hootsworth's Favorite: Paid interest on student loans this year? You can deduct up to $2,500 off your income! Look for Form 1098-E from your loan servicer. You don't have to itemize to get this one!
  • Adjustments = legal ways to reduce your income BEFORE tax is calculated
  • Student loan interest deduction: up to $2,500 — don't miss it!
  • These come from Schedule 1 Part II — we cover that in detail below

🔢 Part 5: Adjusted Gross Income — Line 11

Line 11 is one of the MOST IMPORTANT numbers on your whole return. Your AGI (Adjusted Gross Income) is used to determine what credits you can get, whether you qualify for deductions, and more!

Line 11 — AGI (Adjusted Gross Income) KEY NUMBER!
Line 9 (Total Income) MINUS Line 10 (Adjustments)
This is your "official income" in the eyes of the IRS. It's smaller than what you actually earned (because of the adjustments). Your AGI affects: your standard deduction, tax credits, and eligibility for lots of things.
📌 $38,585 income − $2,500 student loan interest = $36,085 AGI
💡 Remember your AGI! When you file electronically next year, you'll need THIS YEAR's AGI to verify your identity. Write it down and save it!
  • AGI = Total Income − Adjustments
  • This is the number that determines what deductions and credits you qualify for
  • Write it down — you'll need it again next year!

📋 Part 6: Deductions (Line 12)

Another BIG subtraction! You get to reduce your income even MORE with a deduction. Most people take the "standard deduction" — it's a fixed amount the IRS lets everyone subtract automatically. No receipts needed!

🎯 Standard vs. Itemized? Think of it like a restaurant with a "set meal deal" vs. ordering every item separately. The set meal (standard) is usually cheaper... but if you spent a LOT on mortgage interest, medical bills, etc., ordering separately (itemizing) might be bigger!

Line 12 — Standard OR Itemized Deduction
Take the BIGGER of: (A) your standard deduction (from table above), OR (B) your total from Schedule A (itemized deductions). Tax software automatically picks the bigger one for you!
📌 Your standard deduction > your itemized → take the standard. Easy!
  • Most first-time filers take the standard deduction — no receipts needed!
  • The standard deduction increases slightly every year for inflation (automatically)
  • Only itemize if your Schedule A total is BIGGER than the standard

💡 Part 7: Taxable Income — Line 15

After all those subtractions, we finally arrive at your TAXABLE INCOME — the actual number the IRS uses to calculate what you owe. It's WAY smaller than your actual earnings!

Line 15 — Taxable Income KEY!
AGI (Line 11) MINUS your Total Deductions (Line 14)
This is it! If this number is zero or negative, enter $0 — you owe NO federal income tax! 🎉
📌 $36,085 AGI − $15,000 standard deduction = $21,085 taxable income
🧮 The Formula So Far: Gross Income → Minus Adjustments = AGI → Minus Deductions = TAXABLE INCOME → Now we find the tax on THIS number!
  • Taxable income is usually MUCH lower than your actual earnings
  • Zero or negative = zero tax owed!
  • This is the number you look up in the tax tables (or software does it)

💰 Part 8: Tax & Credits (Lines 16–24)

Now we find out your tax — and then the fun part: CREDITS! Tax credits are the BEST thing in taxes because they reduce your tax dollar-for-dollar. A $1,000 credit = $1,000 off your bill!

  • Line 16: Find your tax from the tax table
  • Line 19: Child Tax Credit — up to $2,000 per child!
  • Line 24: Your total tax after all credits
Line 16 — Your Income Tax
Look up your taxable income in the IRS Tax Table (or let software do it). The tax depends on your income AND filing status.
📌 $21,085 taxable income, Single → Tax ≈ $2,300 (at 10–12% bracket)
💡 Current Tax Brackets (Single filer, approximate — auto-updates each year):
10% → income up to about $11,925
12% → $11,926 to $48,475
22% → $48,476 to $103,350
These are the MARGINAL rates — you only pay the higher rate on dollars IN that bracket, not your whole income!
🍕 Tax Brackets are like pizza prices! Imagine the first 2 slices cost $1 each, the next 2 cost $2 each. Your TOTAL for 4 slices is $6 — not $8! You don't pay the higher price on ALL the slices — just the ones in that tier!
Line 19 — Child Tax Credit BIG MONEY!
Up to $2,000 per qualifying child under 17!
Have kids under 17? This credit directly wipes dollars off your tax bill — dollar for dollar! Up to $2,000 per child. Also $500 for other qualifying dependents (parents, adult relatives you support).
📌 2 kids under 17 → $4,000 credit! If your tax is $2,300, you now owe $0 and get $1,700 back!
Line 24 — Total Tax YOUR FINAL TAX BILL
Your tax after all non-refundable credits are applied. Next, we compare this to what you ALREADY paid!
  • Tax is calculated on Line 15 (taxable income) — not your full salary!
  • Credits are BETTER than deductions — they reduce tax dollar-for-dollar
  • Child Tax Credit: up to $2,000 per kid under 17
  • Line 24 = total tax you owe for the year

🏦 Part 9: Payments (Lines 25–33)

You probably ALREADY paid some taxes during the year without realizing it — through paycheck withholding! Now we count it all up and compare to what you owe.

Line 25a — Federal Tax Withheld from W-2 MOST PEOPLE!
Look at your W-2 Box 2 — "Federal income tax withheld." That's how much your employer already sent to the IRS on your behalf all year. This is usually your biggest payment!
📌 W-2 Box 2 = $3,500 → Line 25a = $3,500. You already paid this!
Line 27 — Earned Income Credit (EITC) FREE MONEY!
Up to $8,046 back — even if you owe $0 tax!
This is a REFUNDABLE credit for people who work but don't earn too much. Even if you owe zero tax, the EITC can send money INTO your pocket as a refund! Amount depends on income and number of children. This is one of the most valuable credits out there — don't miss it!
📌 3 kids + income around $30,000 → could get $7,000+ back! Use tax software to calculate your exact amount.
Line 28 — Additional Child Tax Credit (Schedule 8812)
If your Child Tax Credit (Line 19) was more than your tax bill, the leftover can come back to you as a REFUND — up to $1,700 per child!
Line 33 — Total Payments ADD IT ALL UP!
All your withholding + all refundable credits combined. Now we compare this number to Line 24 (what you owe)!
  • W-2 Box 2 = your biggest payment (already sent to IRS by your employer)
  • EITC can give you money BACK even if you owe $0 in tax — always check!
  • Line 33 = everything you've paid + all refundable credits

🎉 Part 10: Refund or Amount You Owe!

THE MOMENT YOU'VE BEEN WAITING FOR! Compare what you owe (Line 24) vs. what you paid (Line 33). If you paid more → REFUND! If you paid less → small amount due. Either way — almost done!

🦉 Professor Hootsworth says: YOU'RE SO CLOSE!

🎉 THE MOMENT OF TRUTH! This is where you find out: REFUND or owe a bit more?

Line 33 BIGGER than Line 24? → 🎉 REFUND coming your way!
Line 24 BIGGER? → Pay the difference. No biggie!

Line 34 — YOUR REFUND! 🎉
Line 33 minus Line 24 = Money the IRS sends YOU!
If you paid MORE than you owed, this is your refund amount. The IRS will direct-deposit this right into your bank account!
📌 Paid $3,500, owed $2,300 → REFUND = $1,200! 🎉
Lines 35b–35d — Direct Deposit Info
Enter your bank routing number and account number to get your refund deposited directly — fastest way (often just 8–21 days after e-filing)! Your routing number is the 9 digits at the bottom left of your check.
Line 37 — Amount You OWE
If you owed MORE than what was withheld, pay this amount by April 15. Go to IRS.gov/pay and use Direct Pay — it's free and takes 2 minutes! Or mail a check to "United States Treasury."
📌 Owed $2,300, only paid $2,000 → Line 37 = $300 due by April 15
  • Paid MORE than you owed → Refund (Line 34)!
  • Paid LESS than you owed → Small payment due (Line 37)
  • Use direct deposit — fastest way to get your money!
  • Pay anything owed by April 15 to avoid interest charges

✍️ Part 11: Sign Your Return!

Almost done! But DON'T FORGET THIS — an unsigned tax return is INVALID. It's like a contract with no signature. The IRS will literally send it back!

✍️ DO NOT FORGET TO SIGN! This is the #1 mistake beginners make. No signature = return rejected!
Your Signature + Date ← REQUIRED!
Sign your name on the signature line. Write today's date. By signing, you're legally saying: "I swear this is accurate." Both spouses must sign if Married Filing Jointly!
Your Occupation
Just write your job: "Student," "Server," "Cashier," "Driver," "Teacher," "Engineer," etc. Be honest — it doesn't affect your taxes!
✅ E-filing? When you file online, your digital PIN replaces your signature. Tax software handles this automatically — just follow the prompts!
  • ALWAYS sign your return — unsigned = invalid!
  • Married filing jointly = BOTH spouses sign
  • E-filing creates a digital signature for you automatically

📄 Schedule 1 — Extra Income & Adjustments

Schedule 1 handles two things: (1) Extra types of income that don't fit on the main form, and (2) Adjustments that reduce your income. Only fill this out if any of these apply to you!

  • Part I: Unemployment, business income, gambling winnings
  • Part II: Student loan interest, IRA deductions, educator expenses

Schedule 1 Part I — Extra Income

Line 7 — Unemployment Compensation SURPRISE — TAXABLE!
YES, unemployment money is taxable!
Got unemployment benefits? The FULL amount is taxable income. You'll find it on Form 1099-G Box 1. Many people forget this and get a surprise bill! Check the box to have taxes withheld if you're currently receiving unemployment.
📌 Got $9,000 in unemployment → Line 7 = $9,000. Tax will be owed on this!
Line 3 — Business Income (from Schedule C)
Self-employed, freelancer, gig worker (Uber, DoorDash, Etsy, babysitting, lawn mowing)? Your net profit from Schedule C goes here.
Line 8a — Gambling/Lottery Winnings
Won money at a casino, lottery, or online gambling? It's taxable! Look for Form W-2G. (The good news: you can deduct gambling losses up to your winnings on Schedule A.)

Schedule 1 Part II — Adjustments (These LOWER Your Income!)

Line 21 — Student Loan Interest Deduction UP TO $2,500!
Paid interest on student loans? Subtract up to $2,500!
Find the amount on Form 1098-E from your loan servicer. This deduction reduces your income DIRECTLY — even if you take the standard deduction. Income limits may apply.
📌 Paid $1,800 in student loan interest → saves you about $216 in taxes (12% bracket)
Line 20 — IRA Deduction
Contributed to a Traditional IRA? Up to $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+) may be deductible, depending on your income and whether you have a workplace retirement plan.
Line 11 — Educator Expenses
K–12 teacher, aide, counselor, or principal? Deduct up to $300 you personally spent on classroom supplies!
  • Unemployment is TAXABLE — report it on Line 7!
  • Gig work income goes here via Schedule C
  • Student loan interest → deduct up to $2,500 (no itemizing needed!)
  • IRA contributions can be deductible too!

📄 Schedule 2 — Additional Taxes

Schedule 2 is for extra taxes beyond regular income tax. Most simple filers skip this entirely! But check if any of these apply to you.

Line 4 — Self-Employment Tax (from Schedule SE)
If you're self-employed, you pay 15.3% for Social Security and Medicare on your business profit. You also get to deduct half of it as an adjustment!
Line 8 — Early IRA Withdrawal Penalty (10%)
Took money from your IRA before age 59½? Add 10% of that amount here as a penalty. Exceptions exist for first home purchase, disability, certain medical costs.
📌 Withdrew $5,000 from IRA at age 35 → $500 penalty on Line 8
  • Most simple filers don't need Schedule 2
  • Self-employed? Your SE tax (15.3%) goes here from Schedule SE
  • Early IRA withdrawal penalty = 10% of the amount taken

📄 Schedule 3 — Extra Credits & Payments

MORE credits! Schedule 3 has additional credits that couldn't fit on the main 1040. These are GOOD — they reduce your tax bill. Check each one!

Line 3 — Education Credits (Form 8863) UP TO $2,500!
American Opportunity Credit — first 4 years of college
Up to $2,500 per year for the first 4 years of college or vocational school. Must be enrolled at least half-time. The BEST part: 40% ($1,000) is REFUNDABLE even if you owe zero tax!
Line 4 — Retirement Savings Credit OFTEN MISSED!
The "Saver's Credit" — up to $1,000 just for saving!
Contributed to a 401(k), IRA, or other retirement account? If your income is below the threshold, you get a credit just for saving! Don't skip this!
Line 2 — Child and Dependent Care Credit
Paid for daycare or summer camp so you could work? Get a credit worth up to 35% of up to $3,000 (one child) or $6,000 (two+ children) in care expenses.
Line 5a — Residential Clean Energy Credit
Installed solar panels, wind energy, or geothermal heating at your home? Get 30% of the cost as a credit — no dollar cap!
  • American Opportunity Credit: up to $2,500 for college students — $1,000 refundable!
  • Saver's Credit: up to $1,000 for contributing to retirement accounts
  • Daycare costs → Child and Dependent Care Credit
  • Solar panels → 30% clean energy credit

📋 Schedule A — Itemized Deductions

Most people take the standard deduction. But if you paid a LOT for mortgage interest, medical bills, or donated a ton to charity, adding up your actual expenses (itemizing) might save you more. Let's check!

🤔 Should you itemize? Add up: mortgage interest + state/property taxes + charitable donations + big medical bills. If that total beats your standard deduction → itemize! If not → take the standard. Simple!
Lines 1–4 — Medical and Dental Expenses
You can deduct medical costs that exceed 7.5% of your AGI. Example: AGI $40,000 × 7.5% = $3,000 threshold. If you paid $5,000 medical → deduct $2,000. Counts: doctor visits, prescriptions, glasses, dental, surgery, insurance premiums (that you pay yourself).
Lines 5–6 — State, Local & Property Taxes (SALT) — Capped at $10,000!
Deduct state income taxes OR sales taxes (pick one) PLUS your property taxes. BUT — the TOTAL SALT deduction is capped at $10,000 per return. If you live in a high-tax state, you'll hit this cap quickly.
Lines 8–9 — Mortgage Interest BIG ONE for homeowners!
Interest you paid on your home mortgage (from Form 1098). For loans taken after Dec 16, 2017, deductible on the first $750,000 of loan principal. Often the BIGGEST itemized deduction!
Lines 11–14 — Charitable Contributions
Money donated to qualified charities (churches, food banks, Red Cross, schools). Cash donations need a receipt or bank statement. Donated clothes to Goodwill? Get a receipt with the estimated value.
📌 $800 to your church + $300 clothes to Goodwill = $1,100 deduction
  • Only itemize if your total is BIGGER than the standard deduction
  • SALT (state taxes + property tax) capped at $10,000
  • Mortgage interest is often the biggest itemized deduction for homeowners
  • Keep receipts for ALL charitable donations!

💰 Schedule B — Interest & Dividends

Schedule B is simple! You only need it if your bank interest OR dividends are each over $1,500. Below that? Just put the totals directly on Form 1040 — no Schedule B needed!

When Do You Need Schedule B?
Only if: Taxable interest from banks/bonds > $1,500 in total, OR ordinary dividends from stocks > $1,500 in total. Under $1,500 each? Just enter the totals on Form 1040 Lines 2b and 3b directly — done!
Part I — Interest
List each bank/institution that paid you interest and the amount. The total flows to Form 1040 Line 2b.
Part II — Ordinary Dividends
List each company or fund that paid you dividends. Total ordinary dividends flow to Form 1040 Line 3b.
  • Under $1,500 in interest AND dividends? No Schedule B needed!
  • Over $1,500? Just list each source with amounts — it's a simple list

💼 Schedule C — Self-Employment & Business Income

Drive for Uber? Sell on Etsy? Do freelance work? Mow lawns for cash? Then Schedule C is for YOU! You report all your business income AND subtract your business expenses. You only pay tax on the PROFIT!

  • Your business income (everything customers paid you)
  • Your business expenses (car mileage, supplies, phone, home office)
  • Net Profit = Income − Expenses → only this gets taxed!
Line 1 — Gross Receipts
ALL money your business received — cash, Venmo, PayPal, checks, 1099-NECs. Every single dollar you earned doing your gig or freelance work.
Part II — Business Expenses SAVE MONEY HERE!
🚗 Line 9: Car/Vehicle — Use the IRS mileage rate (check IRS.gov for current year rate — typically around 67¢/mile) for business driving. Keep a mileage log!

📱 Phone/Internet — Business portion of your phone/internet bill

🏠 Line 30: Home Office — If you use part of your home exclusively for business, you can deduct a portion of rent/mortgage, utilities

🍽️ Line 24b: Business Meals — 50% of meals with clients or while traveling for business

📦 Line 22: Supplies & Materials — Anything you buy specifically for your business
Line 31 — Net Profit or Loss KEY NUMBER!
Gross Receipts MINUS Total Expenses = Your Business Profit
This is what goes to Schedule 1 Line 3 — and what gets taxed! Maximize your legitimate deductions to minimize this number.
📌 Made $25,000 on DoorDash, deducted $9,000 in mileage/expenses → Net profit = $16,000 (this gets taxed)
⚠️ Self-Employed? BIG WARNING: Your profit is taxed as income AND as self-employment tax (15.3%). Set aside 25-30% of every business dollar for taxes! Pay quarterly estimated taxes to avoid penalties!
  • Self-employed/gig worker? This schedule is REQUIRED
  • Track ALL business expenses — every mile, every receipt!
  • Net profit = income - expenses (minimize this legally!)
  • This profit also triggers self-employment tax on Schedule SE

📈 Schedule D — Capital Gains & Losses

Sold stocks, cryptocurrency, or real estate? Schedule D is where you report the profit (or loss). The BIG secret: hold your investment for more than a year and you pay a MUCH lower tax rate!

SHORT-TERM vs. LONG-TERM — This Changes Everything!
📅 Short-term (held 1 year or LESS): Taxed at your regular income tax rate — could be 22%, 24%, or more!

📅 Long-term (held MORE than 1 year): Taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% — much lower! Most people pay 0% or 15%.
📌 Bought crypto Jan 1, sold Dec 1 = SHORT-TERM (higher tax). Sold Jan 15 next year = LONG-TERM (much lower tax)!
Each Sale Needs: Date Acquired, Date Sold, Sale Price, Cost Basis
Your broker sends a 1099-B with all this info. Just transfer it to Schedule D. Tax software does this automatically!
🥈 Silver Lining on Losses! Lost money on investments? Up to $3,000 of capital losses can reduce your other income each year. Unused losses carry forward to future years — forever!
  • Sold stocks/crypto/real estate → use Schedule D
  • Held over 1 year = long-term = lower tax rate
  • Investment losses can offset up to $3,000 of regular income per year

🏠 Schedule E — Rental Income & Partnerships

Rent out a room on Airbnb? Own a rental property? Receive royalties from a book? Own part of a business partnership? Schedule E handles all of this!

Line 3 — Rents Received
ALL rent from tenants — monthly checks, Venmo, cash. Everything a tenant paid you goes here.
Lines 5–18 — Rental Expenses BIG DEDUCTIONS!
These ALL reduce your rental income:
• Advertising to find tenants
• Repairs and maintenance
• Property insurance
• Mortgage interest (the interest portion)
• Property taxes
• Utilities you pay
• Property management fees
• Depreciation (Line 18) — HUGE! Residential rental buildings depreciate over 27.5 years. On a $165,000 building → $6,000/year deduction!
Lines 27–43 — Partnership/S-Corp Income
Own part of a partnership or S-corporation? You receive a Schedule K-1 each year. Transfer the income/loss from K-1 Box 1 here.
  • All rental income is taxable — report every dollar
  • Expenses (repairs, insurance, mortgage interest, taxes) all deductible!
  • Depreciation is one of the biggest rental deductions — don't miss it!
  • Partnership income → comes from Schedule K-1

🌾 Schedule F — Farm Income

Own a farm? Schedule F is just like Schedule C, but specifically designed for farmers. Report ALL farm income and ALL farm expenses — seeds, feed, equipment, vet bills, everything!

Part I — Farm Income
Sales of crops, livestock, dairy products · Cooperative distributions · Government farm program payments · Crop insurance proceeds · Custom hire income (operating farm equipment for others)
Part II — Farm Expenses
Chemicals and fertilizer · Feed purchased for animals · Fuel for farm equipment · Hired farm labor · Land rent · Seeds and plants · Veterinary and medicine costs · Farm equipment repairs · Property taxes on farm land · Depreciation on equipment and buildings
Line 34 — Net Farm Profit or Loss
Farm income minus all farm expenses. Profit goes to Schedule 1 Line 6 and triggers self-employment tax on Schedule SE.
  • Farm income = everything you sold or received from farming
  • Farm expenses = EVERYTHING you spent to run the farm
  • Net profit goes to Schedule 1 and is subject to SE tax

🧾 Schedule SE — Self-Employment Tax

When you work for a company, they pay HALF your Social Security and Medicare taxes for you. When you work for YOURSELF, you pay both halves — 15.3% total. That's what Schedule SE calculates!

🤯 Why 15.3%? Social Security = 12.4% + Medicare = 2.9% = 15.3% total. Normally your employer pays 7.65% and you pay 7.65%. Self-employed? You pay all 15.3%! (BUT you deduct half of it back as an adjustment — fair trade!)
Schedule SE — The Calculation
Step 1: Net profit (from Schedule C/F) × 92.35% = net earnings from self-employment
Step 2: Multiply by 15.3% = your self-employment tax
Step 3: This amount goes on Schedule 2, Line 4
Bonus Step: Deduct HALF of your SE tax on Schedule 1 (reduces your income!)
📌 $16,000 profit × 92.35% = $14,776 × 15.3% = $2,261 SE tax. Deduct $1,130 on Schedule 1!
⚠️ Quarterly Estimated Taxes! Self-employed people must pay taxes 4 times a year (April 15, June 15, Sept 15, Jan 15). If you wait until April to pay it all, you'll owe an underpayment penalty!
  • Self-employed = you pay 15.3% SE tax on top of income tax
  • BUT you deduct half the SE tax as an adjustment — saves you money!
  • Set aside 25-30% of all self-employment income for taxes
  • Pay quarterly estimates to avoid underpayment penalties

📎 Other Key Tax Forms

A few more forms come up regularly. You only fill out the ones that apply to your situation!

Form 8863 — Education Credits

American Opportunity Credit — First 4 Years of College
Up to $2,500 per year. Must be enrolled at least half-time. Qualified costs: tuition, fees, textbooks, required materials. 40% ($1,000 max) is REFUNDABLE — you can get money back even with zero tax!
Lifetime Learning Credit — Any Year of College
20% of first $10,000 in tuition = up to $2,000 credit. Available for ANY year of college, grad school, or job skills courses. NOT refundable.

Form 4868 — Extension of Time to File

Need More Time? File by April 15 → Get Until October 15!
This gives you 6 more months to FILE your return — but NOT more time to PAY! You must estimate and pay any taxes owed by April 15 or you'll owe interest and penalties. File Form 4868 electronically before April 15 — it's automatically approved!

Form 8889 — Health Savings Account (HSA)

HSA Contributions and Distributions
Have a high-deductible health plan with an HSA? Contributions you make yourself are tax-deductible! Withdrawals for medical expenses are tax-free. Limits adjust each year — check IRS.gov for current amounts.

Form 8962 — Premium Tax Credit (Healthcare.gov)

Health Insurance Marketplace Subsidy Reconciliation
Got health insurance through Healthcare.gov? This form reconciles the advance subsidy you received with what you actually qualified for based on your real income. May mean more money back — or some to pay back.
  • College student → check Form 8863 (up to $2,500 credit!)
  • Need more time? File Form 4868 by April 15 — but still pay what you owe!
  • HSA contributions reduce your taxable income
  • Healthcare.gov users → must file Form 8962 to reconcile subsidies
