✅ PART 1 — HOW TO DO TAXES (THE BIG PICTURE)

🦉 How to get ready for taxes

💡 What to do first:

  • Find your W-2 (this shows money from jobs)
  • Get any 1099 forms (side jobs like babysitting)
  • Check your bank account for interest earned
  • Write down any extra money you made
Extra Tips:
  • Put everything in ONE folder (paper or digital)
  • Don’t start until you have all your forms
  • If something is missing, ask early (don’t wait!)
  • Being organized makes everything easier

🦉 How to understand taxes (super simple)

💡 Think of taxes like this:

  • Income = all the money you made
  • Deductions = money that lowers your income
  • Credits = money that lowers your tax (BEST one!)
Extra Tips: Credits can give you money back 💰. Deductions only reduce how much is taxed. You don’t need to understand everything—just these 3.

🦉 How to fill out your tax form

💡 Step-by-step:

  • Start at Line 1
  • Read what it asks
  • Copy numbers from your forms
  • Move to the next line
Extra Tips:
  • Go slowly—this is NOT a race
  • Never guess numbers
  • Use a calculator
  • If a line doesn’t apply, skip it

🦉 How to check your work (VERY IMPORTANT)

💡

  • Re-read every line
  • Check your math
  • Make sure numbers match your forms
Extra Tips:
  • Most mistakes are small (like typing errors)
  • Double-checking can help you get a bigger refund
  • Take a short break, then check again

🦉 How to know if you get a refund or owe money

💡

  • If you paid MORE than needed → 🎉 refund
  • If you paid LESS → you owe

Easy Example:
You paid $50
You only owed $30
👉 You get $20 back

Extra Tips: Refund = your money coming back. Owing money is normal sometimes.

🦉 How to add up your income (Lines 4–8)

💡 Step-by-step:

  • Line 4 – Interest you earned from banks
  • Line 5 – Dividends from stocks or investments
  • Line 6 – IRA distributions or pensions
  • Line 7 – Social Security benefits
  • Line 8 – Other income (gambling, freelance, tips)
Extra Tips:
  • Check each form carefully (1099-INT, 1099-DIV, etc.)
  • Don’t forget side hustles or small jobs
  • Add everything accurately to avoid errors
  • Professor Hootington says: “Think of it like adding all the pieces of a puzzle—you need each piece!”

🦉 How to subtract deductions

  • Standard deduction or itemized deductions
  • Education expenses
  • Charitable donations
Extra Tips: Deductions lower your taxable income. Use whatever is larger: standard deduction or itemized.

🦉 How to apply tax credits

  • Child tax credit
  • Earned income credit
  • Education credits (like the American Opportunity Credit)
Extra Tips: Credits directly reduce your tax bill. Professor Hootington reminds: “Credits = money back in your pocket!”

🦉 How to adjust your income (Line 9)

  • Student loan interest
  • IRA contributions
  • Self-employment expenses
Extra Tips:
  • Adjustments lower your total income, which can lower your tax
  • Professor Hootington says: “Don’t skip these—they can save you money!”

🦉 Calculate your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)

Your AGI = Total income minus adjustments.

Extra Tips: Keep your AGI handy—you’ll use it for deductions, credits, and taxes owed.

🦉 Choose deductions (Lines 11–12)

  • Line 11 – Standard deduction or itemized deduction (Schedule A)
  • Line 12 – Taxable income = AGI minus deductions
Extra Tips:
  • Use the larger deduction to lower your tax
  • Professor Hootington says: “Every dollar counts!”

🦉 Calculate your tax (Lines 13–15)

  • Line 13 – Tax owed based on taxable income
  • Schedule 2 – Additional taxes (self-employment tax, AMT)
  • Line 15 – Total tax = Tax + additional taxes
Extra Tips: Double-check calculations. Use a tax table or software for accuracy.

🦉 Apply credits and payments (Lines 16–20)

  • Line 16 – Federal tax withheld from W-2/1099
  • Line 17 – Estimated tax payments
  • Schedule 3 – Credits (education credits, retirement savings, child tax credit)
  • Line 20 – Total payments and credits
Extra Tips:
  • Credits directly reduce your tax owed
  • Professor Hootington reminds: “Check every credit you qualify for—you might get more back!”

🦉 Determine refund or amount owed (Lines 21–24)

  • If total payments > total tax → refund
  • If total tax > total payments → amount owed
  • Line 24 – Refund amount (if any)
  • Line 23 – Amount you owe (if any)
Extra Tips: Double-check numbers before submitting. Professor Hootington says: “No one wants surprises!”

🦉 Sign and date your return (Line 25)

Line 25 – Don’t forget to sign and date your 1040 before submitting. Unsigned returns are rejected!

Extra Tips:
  • Both spouses must sign if filing jointly
  • Keep a copy for your records

🦉 Supporting schedules & forms

  • Schedule A – Itemized deductions
  • Schedule B – Interest & dividends
  • Schedule C – Business/Self-employment income
  • Schedule D – Capital gains & losses
  • Schedule E – Rental & partnership income
  • Schedule F – Farm income
  • Schedule SE – Self-employment tax
  • Schedule 8812 – Child tax credit
  • Form 8863 – Education credits
  • Form 8880 – Retirement savings contributions credit
Extra Tips: Fill out schedules first, then transfer numbers to your 1040. Professor Hootington says: “Organize your papers before crunching numbers!”

📚 Frequently Asked Questions

1. Can I file my taxes completely online?

Yes! You can file online using IRS Free File or trusted tax software. Click here to file online.

2. What if I made a mistake after filing?

You can file an amended return using Form 1040-X. Correcting errors is important for your tax record.

3. How do I check my refund?

Visit the IRS “Where’s My Refund?” tool. You’ll need your filing status, SSN, and refund amount.

4. Do I need to pay estimated taxes?

Only if you have income not subject to withholding (like freelance work). Use Form 1040-ES to pay quarterly.

5. What’s the difference between standard and itemized deductions?

Standard deduction is a flat amount; itemized deductions include mortgage interest, donations, and other eligible expenses. Choose whichever lowers your tax the most.

Step 13 — Other Taxes

💬 Professor Hootington says: “Some additional taxes may apply, like the alternative minimum tax (AMT) or the net investment income tax.”

  • Line 15 – Additional Taxes: Includes AMT, net investment income tax, and other special taxes.

Step 14 — Your Refund or Balance Due

💬 Professor Hootington says: “Once all the calculations are done, this is where you see if you're getting a refund or owe money.”

  • If your payments (including withholding) are greater than your total tax, you'll get a refund!
  • If your total tax is higher than what you've already paid, you’ll owe money to the IRS.
